01 / 18 — Introduction

A rescuer that never gets tired, never hesitates, and never has to swim into danger first.

Every year, drowning incidents at beaches, rivers, dams, and flood zones put untrained bystanders and first responders at direct risk the moment they enter the water themselves. This project presents a remotely operated rescue boat — a compact, battery-powered surface vessel controlled from shore, fitted with a live FPV (first-person-view) camera link, that can be launched in seconds and driven directly to a person in distress without a human rescuer ever leaving dry ground.

The system combines marine hull design, twin-motor propulsion, a radio control link, and a real-time video downlink into a single low-cost platform intended for lifeguard stations, disaster-response teams, and coastal safety units.

02 / 18 — Problem Statement

The most dangerous part of a water rescue is often the rescuer entering the water.

Manual water rescues expose responders to the same currents, fatigue, and panic-driven grabbing that endangered the original victim — a well-documented cause of secondary drowning incidents involving would-be rescuers. Response time is also constrained by how quickly a trained swimmer can reach the scene, and visibility from shore drops sharply in choppy water, at night, or across long distances.

  • Rescuers face direct physical risk entering unpredictable water conditions.
  • Line-of-sight observation from shore is unreliable at range or in low light.
  • Boat-based rescue crews require staffing, fuel, and response coordination time.
  • Existing rescue equipment (buoys, ropes) has limited reach and no active propulsion.

This project asks: can a small, inexpensive, remotely piloted vessel close that gap between "someone is drowning" and "help has arrived"?

04 / 18 — Existing Solutions

What's already out there — and where it falls short.

Manned rescue boats

Fast and capable, but require a trained crew, fuel, mooring, and minutes of response time — and still place a human on the water.

Throw-line & rescue buoys

Simple and cheap, but limited by throwing distance and offer no active propulsion once released.

Aerial rescue drones

Excellent overview footage and fast deployment, but cannot physically support a victim's weight or tow them to shore.

Commercial RC lifebuoys

The closest analogue to this project — motorised flotation devices exist commercially, but are typically single-purpose, costly, and lack an integrated FPV feed for precision piloting in poor visibility.

The gap this project targets: a platform that is driven like a vehicle rather than thrown like an object, piloted by sight rather than guesswork, and inexpensive enough for a single lifeguard station to own outright.

05 / 18 — Our Solution

A twin-hull FPV rescue vessel, piloted like a vehicle, deployed like a life ring.

The proposed system pairs a lightweight catamaran-style hull with twin brushless thrusters, a stabilised FPV camera, and a rescue-grade flotation collar around the bow. An operator on shore watches the live video feed on a monitor or FPV goggles and drives the boat directly alongside the victim, who grips the flotation collar while the pilot brings them back to shallow water.

06 / 18 — Boat Overview

The complete platform, at a glance.

The hull is a shallow-draft twin-pontoon design chosen for stability over speed — the boat needs to stay upright while a frightened victim grabs and pulls on it. All electronics sit in a sealed deck box above the waterline, isolated from the pump and motor wiring below.

10 / 18 — Electronics

The nervous system beneath the deck.

A microcontroller-based flight controller reads the RF receiver and translates stick input into differential thrust between the two motors, allowing the boat to spin tightly on the spot. Electronic speed controllers (ESCs) regulate motor power from the shared LiPo bus, while a buck converter steps power down for the camera and receiver.

11 / 18 — FPV Camera System

Seeing through the boat's eyes, not the shoreline's.

A forward-facing, wide-angle camera sits on a shock-mounted bracket above the bow, feeding a low-latency analog video transmitter. The pilot watches this feed on FPV goggles or a ground-station monitor, giving a first-person perspective that keeps the victim centred in frame even in waves or low light.

12 / 18 — Remote Controller

Two sticks. Zero training curve.

The handheld transmitter uses a familiar dual-stick layout — one axis for throttle and steering, the other reserved for the pump and flotation-release toggle — so a lifeguard can operate it confidently after a five-minute briefing.

13 / 18 — Water Pump

A small pump with an outsized job.

A submersible bilge-style pump serves two roles: keeping the sealed hull compartments dry, and — via a diverter valve — powering a jet outlet that can nudge a conscious victim toward the flotation collar without the hull making direct contact.

14 / 18 — Battery

Enough charge to matter, light enough to float.

A single high-discharge LiPo pack powers propulsion, electronics, the camera link, and the pump from one balanced bus, with a low-voltage cutoff that protects cell health and warns the pilot well before the boat loses thrust.

15 / 18 — Propulsion System

Twin thrusters, tight turns, guarded props.

Independent brushless thrusters in shrouded, finger-safe housings give the boat tank-style steering — full-speed reverse on one side and forward on the other lets it rotate in place to keep the camera trained on a moving victim.

17 / 18 — Future Improvements

What version two looks like.

  • GPS waypoint return-to-shore mode for signal-loss failsafe.
  • Computer-vision assisted victim tracking to keep the camera locked on target.
  • Thermal imaging for night and low-visibility search operations.
  • Digital, encrypted video and control links for longer, more reliable range.
  • Multi-boat swarm coordination for large-scale search patterns.

18 / 18 — Conclusion

A small boat, built to make sure everyone comes home.

This project demonstrates that a remotely operated FPV rescue boat is a practical, affordable way to shorten the most dangerous minutes of a water emergency — removing the rescuer from the hazard, not the rescue from the response. The completed prototype validates the core hypothesis: distance and visibility no longer have to stand between a person in trouble and the help on its way.
